## I. The Null Point

A null point is a location in a complex scalar field $\psi(\mathbf{r}, t)$ where the amplitude vanishes ($|\psi| = 0$) and the phase $\arg(\psi)$ is undefined. The phase winds by $2\pi q$ around each null, where $q \in \mathbb{Z}$ is the topological charge:

$$q = \frac{1}{2\pi} \oint_C \nabla \arg(\psi) \cdot d\mathbf{l}$$

Null points carry quantized topological charge $q = \pm 1$. They are created and destroyed only in opposite-charge pairs ($+1, -1$), conserving total charge exactly (Nye and Berry, Proc. Roy. Soc. A 336, 165–190, 1974). This conservation is topological — it holds in any continuous complex field regardless of the underlying physics: optics, acoustics, fluid dynamics, superconductors, gravitational waves.

Berry (J. Phys. A 11, 27–37, 1978) predicted that null-point velocities diverge near creation and annihilation events: as a $+1/-1$ pair approaches mutual annihilation, both null points accelerate without bound. This acceleration is superluminal — the null points exceed the propagation speed of the host wave — without violating relativity, because null points carry no mass, no energy, and no information. They are structural features of the field's topology, not physical objects.

Kaminer et al. (Nature 651, 920–926, March 2026) confirmed this prediction experimentally. Using ultrafast electron microscopy at the Technion Electron Microscopy Center, the team tracked optical phase singularities in polariton waves in hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) with sub-wavelength spatial and sub-cycle temporal resolution. Twenty-nine percent of tracked null points exceeded the speed of light in vacuum. The average superluminal speed was $\sim 1.04c$. Near annihilation events, velocities became unbounded, precisely matching Berry's 1978 kinematics.

The Technion result is not restricted to optics. Kaminer's group stated explicitly: "Our discovery reveals universal laws of nature shared by all types of waves, from sound waves and fluid flows to complex systems such as superconductors." The mathematical structure — topological charge conservation, pair creation/annihilation, superluminal null-point motion — is field-independent. It follows from the topology of complex scalar fields, not from the specific equations governing any particular wave.

## II. Gravitational Null Points

General relativity describes spacetime through a metric tensor $g_{\mu\nu}$. A Killing vector field $\xi^\mu$ generates a spacetime symmetry. For stationary spacetimes, the timelike Killing field $\xi^\mu = (\partial/\partial t)^\mu$ has norm $\xi_\mu \xi^\mu = g_{tt}$.

A black hole event horizon is the null surface where this norm vanishes:

$$\xi_\mu \xi^\mu = 0$$

At this surface, the field that normally points "forward in time" degenerates. The distinction between space and time breaks down. The event horizon is a null point of the timelike Killing field in the same precise sense that an optical vortex is a null point of the electromagnetic field amplitude: both are loci where a defining field quantity drops to zero and a phase-like variable becomes undefined.

The Schwarzschild black hole has a point singularity at $r = 0$. The Kerr rotating black hole has a ring singularity at $r = 0, \theta = \pi/2$ — topologically $S^1$, a closed vortex line in spacetime (Kerr, Phys. Rev. Lett. 11, 237–238, 1963; Carter, Phys. Rev. 174, 1559–1571, 1968). This ring singularity is structurally identical to a vortex ring in fluid dynamics or a phase dislocation line in optics (Nye-Berry 1974). The Kerr ring is traversable: a geodesic can pass through the ring without encountering infinite tidal forces, entering a causally distinct region of spacetime.

The topological charge of a black hole — its irreducible mass, angular momentum, and electric charge (the Kerr-Newman parameters) — is conserved, exactly as the topological charge of an optical vortex is conserved. Black holes can only form or merge in processes that respect the area theorem ($\delta A \geq 0$, Hawking 1971) and cosmic censorship (singularities hidden behind horizons, Penrose 1969). These are topological conservation laws governing the gravitational null-point network.

The surface gravity $\kappa$ at the horizon — the gravitational analogue of the "phase gradient" at a null point — determines the Hawking temperature $T_H = \hbar\kappa / (2\pi k_B c)$ (Hawking, Commun. Math. Phys. 43, 199–220, 1975; Gibbons and Hawking, Phys. Rev. D 15, 2738, 1977). The Bekenstein-Hawking entropy $S_{\text{BH}} = A / (4G_N \hbar)$ counts the microscopic degrees of freedom associated with the null surface — the gravitational analogue of the information content encoded in the vortex's topological charge.

## III. The Connected Null-Space Network

### ER = EPR

Einstein and Rosen (Phys. Rev. 48, 73–77, 1935) showed that the Schwarzschild solution contains a "bridge" connecting two exterior regions through the black hole interior — the Einstein-Rosen bridge. Separately, Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen (Phys. Rev. 47, 777–780, 1935) identified quantum entanglement as a nonlocal correlation between distant particles.

Maldacena and Susskind (Fortschritte der Physik 61(9), 781–811, 2013) proposed that these are the same phenomenon: ER = EPR. Two entangled black holes are connected by an Einstein-Rosen bridge. The entanglement entropy between the two systems equals the Ryu-Takayanagi entropy:

$$S_{\text{ent}} = \frac{\text{Area}(\gamma_A)}{4G_N \hbar}$$

where $\gamma_A$ is the minimal codimension-2 bulk surface anchored to the boundary region $A$ (Ryu and Takayanagi, Phys. Rev. Lett. 96, 181602, 2006).

The radical extension: not only black holes but all entangled systems are connected by Planckian wormholes. Maldacena and Susskind: "Even for an entangled pair of particles, in a quantum theory of gravity there must be a Planckian bridge between them, albeit a very quantum mechanical bridge which probably cannot be described by classical geometry."

If correct, the universe is saturated with micro-wormholes at the Planck scale ($\sim 10^{-35}$ m). Every entangled pair — and quantum field theory guarantees maximal vacuum entanglement across the Rindler horizon — is connected by an ER bridge. The null-space network is this Planckian wormhole network: a sub-geometric topology that cannot be directly observed because the individual bridges are non-traversable and carry no information.

Van Raamsdonk (JHEP 2010(6), 1–28, 2010) showed that reducing entanglement between two CFT regions causes the corresponding bulk spacetime to disconnect — the wormhole pinches off. Remove all entanglement and spacetime falls apart. The null-space network is not merely correlated with spacetime — it constitutes spacetime. Spatial connectivity is entanglement connectivity.

### The Growing Interior

The null-space network is not static. The interior geometry of Einstein-Rosen bridges evolves with time. Susskind proposed that the interior volume of a black hole grows linearly even after thermal equilibrium, tracking the computational complexity of the quantum state ("Complexity = Volume," Stanford, Phys. Rev. D 93, 064025, 2016).

Recent work on "Einstein-Rosen caterpillars" (2025) formalized this: as black hole systems evolve under random dynamics, their connecting wormholes become longer, more irregular, and more complex. A "length-randomness correspondence" shows that the geometric length of a wormhole increases with the degree of quantum randomness of the underlying state — complexity equals geometry.

The null-space network computes. It scrambles. It grows. At the rate set by the Maldacena-Shenker-Stanford chaos bound $\lambda_L \leq 2\pi k_B T / \hbar$ (JHEP 08(2016)106), which limits how fast any thermal quantum system can scramble information. Black holes saturate this bound — they are the fastest scramblers in nature (Sekino and Susskind, JHEP 10, 065, 2008).

## IV. Markov Blankets on the Null-Point Manifold

Each null point in a complex scalar field has a local causal neighborhood. Define the Markov blanket $B_i$ of null point $i$ as the minimal set of neighboring null points such that the future trajectory of $i$ is conditionally independent of the global field given $B_i$:

$$P(r_i(t + \Delta t) \mid B_i) = P(r_i(t + \Delta t) \mid \text{global field})$$

The blanket state vector $s_i = \{d_{ij}, q_j, \nabla\arg(\psi)|_{r_j}\}_{j \in B_i}$ captures the distances, topological charges, and local phase gradients of the blanket members. This structure ensures that the dynamics of each null point — including the Berry $-1/2$ velocity scaling near creation/annihilation events — are fully determined by the blanket, with no information leaking in from outside.

For the gravitational null-space network, this has a precise meaning. A black hole's event horizon is a causal boundary: the interior is causally disconnected from the exterior except through the Hawking radiation encoded in the horizon's quantum state. The Markov blanket of a black hole IS its event horizon — the minimal surface that renders the interior conditionally independent of the exterior. The ER bridges connecting black holes pass through the blanket, mediating entanglement without transmitting information. The conditional independence $P(\text{interior} \mid \text{horizon}) = P(\text{interior} \mid \text{universe})$ is the information-theoretic statement of the no-hair theorem: the black hole interior is determined by its horizon state (mass, spin, charge), not by the detailed configuration of the external universe.

This is the holographic principle (Susskind, J. Math. Phys. 36, 6377–6396, 1995; 't Hooft 1993) in Markov blanket language: the boundary (horizon) screens the bulk (interior) from the environment. The Ryu-Takayanagi formula computes the entanglement entropy across the Markov blanket. The Bekenstein-Hawking entropy counts the blanket's degrees of freedom.

## V. Fisher Information on the Singular Manifold

The manifold of null-point configurations — positions, charges, and phase gradients of all null points in a field — carries a natural information metric. Construct a local Fisher-like information matrix $F$ from the phase gradients within each Markov blanket:

$$F_{ij} = \left\langle \frac{\partial \ln L}{\partial s_i} \frac{\partial \ln L}{\partial s_j} \right\rangle$$

where $L$ is the likelihood of the observed null-point configuration given the blanket state. The Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse $F^+$ handles the degenerate directions:

$$F^+ g = U_r \Sigma_r^{-1} U_r^T g$$

where $U_r$ spans the column space of $F$ (directions with nonzero Fisher information) and $U_n$ spans the null space (directions with zero information). The null-space component receives zero response — no update is applied in directions where the field carries no information about the null-point configuration.

This decomposition has a direct gravitational interpretation. The column space col$(F)$ corresponds to directions in the null-point manifold where the gravitational field carries measurable information — the observable degrees of freedom (mass, spin, charge of each black hole). The null space ker$(F)$ corresponds to the interior degrees of freedom — the behind-the-horizon states that are screened by the Markov blanket. The pseudoinverse ensures that the gravitational response of the null-space network acts only along observable directions, with the interior degrees of freedom contributing through entanglement (the ER bridges) but not through direct measurement.

Diagnostics on the Fisher metric track the topological evolution of the null-point network:

The Fisher trace rate $\Xi_F(t) = [\text{Tr}(F_t) - \text{Tr}(F_{t-1})] / \text{Tr}(F_{t-1})$ measures the rate at which the network's total information content changes. Stable configurations stabilize near a characteristic rate.

Rank crossings — events where the rank of $F$ increases by one — signal topological phase transitions: a new null-point pair is created (increasing the network's degrees of freedom) or an existing pair annihilates (decreasing them). Each rank crossing corresponds to a topological charge creation or annihilation event in the wave field.

## VI. From Entanglement Entropy to Dark Matter

### The Proposal

The null-space network's entanglement structure has gravitational consequences beyond the black holes themselves. If spacetime connectivity is entanglement connectivity (Van Raamsdonk 2010), then the entanglement entropy distribution across the null-space network determines the effective gravitational field at all scales — not just at event horizons.

Define the entanglement entropy profile $S_{\text{EE}}(r)$ around a central null point (a black hole of mass $M$) as the von Neumann entropy of the reduced density matrix for the quantum fields inside radius $r$:

$$S_{\text{EE}}(r) = -\text{Tr}(\rho_A \log \rho_A), \quad \rho_A = \text{Tr}_B \, \rho_{\text{total}}$$

where $A$ is the interior of the sphere of radius $r$ and $B$ is the exterior. The Ryu-Takayanagi formula gives $S_{\text{EE}}(r) = \text{Area}(\gamma_r) / (4G_N\hbar)$ where $\gamma_r$ is the minimal surface at radius $r$.

The proposal: the effective dark matter density is the Laplacian of the entanglement entropy profile:

$$\rho_{\text{DM}}(r) \propto \nabla^2 S_{\text{EE}}(r)$$

The physical logic: if $S_{\text{EE}}(r)$ is proportional to the effective gravitational potential $\Phi(r)$ (as suggested by the Jacobson derivation of Einstein's equations from thermodynamics, Phys. Rev. Lett. 75, 1260–1263, 1995), then Poisson's equation $\nabla^2 \Phi = 4\pi G \rho$ gives $\rho \propto \nabla^2 S_{\text{EE}}$.

### The NFW Profile as Entanglement Profile

The Navarro-Frenk-White density profile (ApJ 462, 563–575, 1996) describes the universal dark matter halo structure observed in N-body simulations and inferred from galaxy rotation curves:

$$\rho_{\text{NFW}}(r) = \frac{\rho_0}{(r/r_s)(1 + r/r_s)^2}$$

with $\rho \propto r^{-1}$ at small $r$ (the cusp) and $\rho \propto r^{-3}$ at large $r$ (the falloff). The prediction: the entanglement entropy profile around a Schwarzschild black hole in asymptotically flat spacetime, computed via the generalized Ryu-Takayanagi formula (Engelhardt and Wall, JHEP 2015; Dong, JHEP 2014), produces a $\nabla^2 S_{\text{EE}}$ that matches the NFW functional form — with the scale radius $r_s$ and central density $\rho_0$ determined by the black hole mass $M$ and the entanglement structure of the surrounding null-space network.

### What This Would Replace

If $\rho_{\text{DM}}(r) \propto \nabla^2 S_{\text{EE}}(r)$ reproduces NFW profiles with the correct $r_s$ and $\rho_0$ for observed galaxies, dark matter is not a substance. It is a gravitational effect of the null-space network's topology — the curvature of spacetime produced by the entanglement entropy distribution, with no new particles required. Galaxy rotation curves, gravitational lensing, the CMB acoustic peaks, and the cosmic web would all emerge from the entanglement structure of the vacuum in the presence of the gravitational null-point network.

### What Remains To Be Computed

The Ryu-Takayanagi formula is established in AdS/CFT. Real galaxies are not in AdS space. The "generalized Ryu-Takayanagi formula" for asymptotically flat spacetime is an active research frontier. No one has computed $S_{\text{EE}}(r)$ for a realistic galaxy with a central supermassive black hole and compared to observed rotation curves. This computation is the critical test. Until it is performed, the proposal $\rho_{\text{DM}} \propto \nabla^2 S_{\text{EE}}$ is a prediction, not a result.

## VII. Gravitational Wave Null Points

Gravitational waves in linearized general relativity are described by the strain $h = h_+ + ih_\times$, a complex scalar field on the two-sphere of sky directions. This field satisfies the conditions for Nye-Berry null-point theory: it is complex-valued, continuously varying, and subject to interference. Its null points — locations where $h_+ = h_\times = 0$ simultaneously — are gravitational wave phase singularities carrying quantized topological charge.

### Predictions for LIGO/Virgo/KAGRA

**Prediction 1: Superluminal motion near mergers.** During binary black hole mergers, the gravitational wave strain pattern has complex interference structure with multiple null points. Berry's kinematics predict their velocities become superluminal near the merger peak — the gravitational analogue of the Technion result. In the $\sim$10 ms surrounding the strain peak, the null points of $h_+ + ih_\times$ should exhibit unbounded acceleration as they approach pair annihilation. This is testable in the ringdown waveforms from GWTC-3 and GWTC-4 catalogs.

**Prediction 2: Velocity scaling $\alpha \approx -1/2$ in the stochastic background.** The stochastic gravitational wave background (SGWB) — the superposition of unresolved sources across the universe — is a complex random wave field. Its null-point velocity distribution should follow Berry's $v \propto |q|^{1/2}$ scaling with exponent $\alpha \approx -1/2$. This is testable in cross-correlation analyses of LIGO-Hanford and LIGO-Livingston strain data during O4/O5 observing runs, via wavelet decomposition of the cross-correlation spectrogram.

**Prediction 3: Null density linear in $\Omega_{\text{GW}}(f)$.** The number density of null points per unit time-frequency area in the wavelet-transformed SGWB should scale linearly with the gravitational wave energy density spectrum $\Omega_{\text{GW}}(f)$. A stronger background produces more interference and therefore more null points. This provides an independent estimator of $\Omega_{\text{GW}}(f)$ via topological counting — complementing the standard cross-correlation method.

**Prediction 4: Charge conservation.** Total topological charge in any compact region of the time-frequency plane should be conserved to high precision ($< 1\%$ deviation). Violations would indicate non-Gaussianity in the SGWB or systematic artifacts in the detector data.

## VIII. Cosmic-Scale Predictions

**Prediction 5: SMBH connectivity and the cosmic web.** If supermassive black holes are the high-connectivity nodes of the null-space network, their spatial distribution should correlate with the filamentary structure of the cosmic web beyond what baryonic physics alone predicts. Cross-correlating SMBH catalogs (eROSITA, JWST) with Lyman-$\alpha$ forest tomography (DESI, Subaru PFS) at scales between the virial radius and the Hubble horizon would test whether ER bridge connectivity produces excess clustering.

**Prediction 6: Entanglement entropy bound on halo mass.** If dark matter is the gravitational effect of vacuum entanglement, the total effective dark matter mass inside radius $R$ around a black hole of mass $M$ should satisfy $M_{\text{DM}}(R) \leq S_{\text{BH}} \cdot (R/r_s)$ where $S_{\text{BH}} = A/(4G_N\hbar)$ is the Bekenstein-Hawking entropy. This is a new bound relating black hole entropy to halo mass — testable by comparing observed halo-to-SMBH mass ratios across the $M_{\text{BH}}$-$M_{\text{halo}}$ scaling relation.

**Prediction 7: CMB polarization vortices.** The CMB polarization field ($Q + iU$ Stokes parameters) is a complex scalar field containing phase singularities. Their pair correlation statistics should follow the Nye-Berry universal distribution. Additional correlations imprinted by the null-space network would appear as anomalous clustering of CMB polarization vortices at scales corresponding to the ER bridge network's characteristic connectivity length — testable with Simons Observatory and CMB-S4 polarization maps.

**Prediction 8: NFW parameter anomalies from entanglement topology.** If $\rho_{\text{DM}} \propto \nabla^2 S_{\text{EE}}$, then systematic deviations in the NFW parameters ($\rho_0, r_s$) across different galaxy types should correlate with differences in the entanglement topology of their null-space neighborhoods — specifically, with the number and mass distribution of nearby SMBHs. Galaxies in dense environments (clusters) should show different NFW parameters from isolated galaxies, traceable to the denser null-space network in clusters. This is testable against the SPARC database ($\sim$175 galaxies with measured rotation curves and baryonic mass distributions).
